# Proposal

## Understanding Effective Deepfake Mitigation Methods

## Dalia L. Zamora | Information Technology

### Background

In the last few years, the rapid advancement of Artificial Intelligence has both directly and indirectly influenced much of the content seen online today. In particular, generative AI technologies have propelled deepfakes forward in terms of frequency. Being able to replicate convincing media of any kind can cause enormous damage-several industries are vulnerable to this kind of technology. This could include creating a political campaign around manipulating video/images or falsifying information, as well as explicit or illegal imagery of unknowing individuals (Abdel-Wahab). In addition to reputational damage is financial one(s), such as an incident which led to the loss of 25 million USD from a single video (Shetye et al).

Deepfakes are typically created through people maliciously using generative AI that has the ability to produce realistic audio, video, and photo, but the methods in which each respective media form is generated varies. Some are more common than others; identity/face-swapping, face reenactment, and lip-syncing being the most apparent (Naitali et al). Though to many they can seem too realistic to tell apart from reality, it is still possible to dissect abnormalities in media being consumed. However, it may be very time consuming and/or labor resource intensive, principally if one would like to prevent AI-generated deepfakes on a large-scale platform. This highlights the importance in developing and maintaining reliable deepfake detection methods, especially as the technology continues to advance and evolve over time. With the detections and mitigation methods that we use currently, we are only merely able to keep up with rapid change and reacting to everything newly emerging (O Awodiji and Owoyemi). Among these methods are Forensic Artifact Analysis, Multi-Modal Analysis, 3D Facial Modeling & Dynamic Analysis, and Attention-Based & Deep Learning Techniques (Shetye et al). Due to the nature of continuous technological advances, other approaches to deepfake mitigation may be considered outside of technical mitigation, such as improved public awareness and education (Anwar). With several options, it is best to weigh each one carefully and truly understand what can be gained from each method through research.

### Methodology

To understand the scope of the issue, data will be collected through a lab study open to all majors, and participants must be above 18 years of age to participate.

For this study, 20 people will be recruited through flyers put up in the PSY, MSB, CB1, and CB2 buildings with authorization of their respective building liaisons, as well as digital flyers in the Student Union and John C. Hitt Library. When in contact with the researcher from the flyer, the participant will set up a time to meet in-person to complete the study.

The 20 participants will be divided into two groups of ten: one "informed" and one "uninformed" group. First, both groups will be asked to complete a structured yes-or-no questionnaire in which they will rate how generally confident they are in recognizing AI deepfaked media from one to five (one being "not confident at all" and five being "fully confident"). They will then select whether or not they believe a piece of media to be deepfaked, additionally including a one to five confidence rating for each answer.

Then, The "informed" group will receive informational content surrounding deepfake media and detection methods. Informational content is provided through a 10-15 minute presentation given by the researcher, and questions may be asked relating to any given information during the presentation. Following this, participants will be asked to re-take the questionnaire again after learning these methods.

The "uninformed" group will not be given a presentation and will instead simply complete the second questionnaire directly after the first. In both groups, the researcher will observe and note down the time taken for each question to be answered per participant. Additionally, after each questionnaire is completed, the researcher will ask/interview the participant directly about their thought process and possible methods they may have utilized-conciously or subconsciously-while answering the questions. It is important to note that the researcher should not go out of their way to mention methods or their own observations to the participant, answers should be natural and not influenced by the researcher.

Once their sessions with the researcher are complete, the participants will be given \$10 in cash.

### Anticipated Outcomes

This research will produce both qualitative and quantitative data sets that can be used in academic journals, reports, and papers, among other forms of academic literature. Qualitative provided will include accuracy rate per group, recognition confidence level, and how it does or does not relate to being informed on the content. Quantitative data will be extracted from the interview portion, noting any common methods, behaviors, or thought processes.

### Significance

By understanding the current state of deepfake technologies and how well we are able to create a distinction between real and fabricated/generated media, we are able to prevent or lessen fraud and misinformation in several industries at the benefit of everyday consumers of media; the general public. To complement this, findings may also be used for understanding what is effective or ineffective in terms of diminution towards the effects of deepfake technology.
